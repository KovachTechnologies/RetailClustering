**Introduction to Customer Identity Resolution via Purchase Embeddings**

Customer identity resolution in retail environments remains a core challenge for unifying fragmented transaction records across in-store, online, and omnichannel touchpoints. Anonymous or partially identified purchases—often captured via loyalty cards, payment tokens, or session IDs—must be linked to persistent customer profiles to enable accurate segmentation, personalization, and lifetime-value modeling. Traditional rule-based or heuristic matching (e.g., via email or phone) fails under data sparsity and privacy constraints. The proposed machine-learning pipeline addresses this by deriving dense vector representations of customers directly from their purchase histories, treating each basket as a composition of product embeddings. By embedding products across textual (title, description) and visual (image) modalities, and subsequently fusing these representations, the approach captures semantic and perceptual similarity at scale. Customer vectors are then formed by aggregating product-level embeddings, enabling cosine-distance comparisons or neural clustering to reveal latent spending patterns and resolve identities with high precision. This report details the technical architecture, embedding strategies, aggregation mechanisms, and downstream clustering methods, grounded in established multimodal representation learning techniques.

**Generation of Unimodal Product Embeddings**

Product embeddings begin with unimodal encoders tailored to each data type. For product titles and descriptions, transformer-based language models such as BERT variants or Sentence Transformers produce fixed-dimensional vectors (typically 768 or 1024 dimensions) that encode semantic content, including brand, category, attributes, and usage context. Title embeddings emphasize concise, high-signal phrases, while description embeddings incorporate longer-form narrative, enabling nuanced differentiation between similar items. Pre-training on large corpora followed by domain-adaptive fine-tuning on retail catalogs ensures robustness to domain-specific vocabulary. Image embeddings, in contrast, derive from vision transformers (ViT) or convolutional backbones such as ResNet-50, extracting visual features like color, texture, shape, and style. These are projected into a comparable latent space, often via a linear head to match the dimensionality of text embeddings. Both unimodal streams operate independently during initial encoding, allowing modular experimentation and fallback when one modality is missing (e.g., no image for legacy SKUs). Recent benchmarks confirm that contrastive pre-trained vision models outperform purely supervised CNNs in e-commerce retrieval tasks, particularly when images exhibit high intra-class variance.

**Cross-Modal and Multimodal Fusion Strategies**

To exploit complementary information, the pipeline explicitly constructs cross-embeddings across the three unimodal sources. Late fusion concatenates or averages normalized unimodal vectors (e.g., title + description + image), followed by a lightweight projection layer to learn joint representations. Early fusion feeds interleaved inputs—such as image patches alongside tokenized title and description—into a unified transformer, as demonstrated by natively multimodal models like Gemini Embedding 2, which maps text, images, and documents into a single shared space. Contrastive objectives, exemplified by CLIP-style training, align image and text pairs via InfoNCE loss, pulling matching product modalities together while pushing negatives apart. For richer combinations, Siamese two-tower architectures or triplet-loss networks project title-image pairs into a common space, enabling mismatch detection and fine-grained similarity. Bilinear interaction layers or cross-attention mechanisms further model pairwise modality interactions, capturing fine details such as how a textual attribute (“matte finish”) modulates visual appearance. Empirical results from e-commerce deployments show that multimodal fusion consistently outperforms unimodal baselines, achieving accuracy gains of 5–10% in product similarity tasks by compensating for modality-specific noise. These fused embeddings serve as the atomic unit for downstream customer profiling.

**Customer Profile Construction from Purchase Histories**

Given a customer’s transaction history, individual product embeddings are aggregated into a stable customer vector. Simple mean or weighted pooling (weighted by spend, quantity, or recency) across all purchased items yields a centroid representation that encodes typical basket semantics. For sequential patterns, recurrent architectures or transformer-based basket encoders (e.g., treating purchases as a time-ordered sequence) generate context-aware profiles that distinguish habitual versus exploratory spending. Normalization to unit length ensures rotation-invariant comparisons. In practice, this aggregation step transforms sparse, high-cardinality transaction logs into compact, dense vectors suitable for large-scale similarity search. When multiple transactions per customer are available, hierarchical aggregation—first at basket level, then across baskets—preserves both micro-patterns (single-trip coherence) and macro-patterns (long-term preferences). The resulting customer embeddings lie in the same space as product embeddings, enabling direct nearest-neighbor retrieval for identity linking across anonymous sessions.

**Similarity Measurement and Clustering Approaches**

Cosine distance provides the simplest yet highly effective metric for comparing customer vectors, as it normalizes for magnitude and focuses purely on angular alignment in embedding space. Threshold-based matching or k-nearest-neighbor search (facilitated by approximate nearest-neighbor libraries such as FAISS) identifies probable duplicate identities with sub-second latency at million-scale catalogs. For deeper pattern discovery, neural clustering algorithms operate directly on the embedding manifold. Deep Embedded Clustering (DEC) jointly optimizes an autoencoder reconstruction loss with a target distribution that sharpens cluster assignments, outperforming traditional k-means on non-convex manifolds. Variants incorporating variational autoencoders (VAE) or graph convolutional networks further model uncertainty and relational structure among customers. Hierarchical agglomerative clustering or density-based methods (DBSCAN) serve as robust baselines when cluster count is unknown. Evaluation relies on internal metrics such as silhouette score and Davies-Bouldin index, supplemented by downstream business KPIs including identity-resolution precision-recall and lift in targeted campaign response rates. Recent work on customer embedding clustering confirms that neural approaches recover more coherent behavioral segments than Euclidean k-means alone.

**Application to Identity Resolution and Spending Pattern Identification**

The unified embedding space directly supports identity resolution by computing pairwise cosine similarities between transaction vectors and known customer profiles; matches above a calibrated threshold link anonymous purchases without explicit identifiers. Spending patterns emerge naturally from cluster centroids: one cluster may concentrate on premium electronics with high image-driven style embeddings, while another emphasizes value-oriented groceries signaled by textual price sensitivity. Temporal extensions—appending time-decayed embeddings or recurrent updates—enable dynamic profiling that adapts to evolving preferences. In production, a vector database stores both product and customer embeddings, supporting real-time inference pipelines. This architecture has demonstrated success in analogous retail settings, where multimodal item embeddings improved recommendation relevance and cross-session continuity.

**Implementation Considerations and Scalability**

End-to-end deployment leverages cloud-native services for embedding generation (e.g., Vertex AI multimodal APIs or self-hosted CLIP towers) and vector indexing (Pinecone, OpenSearch k-NN). Batch inference on GPU clusters handles catalog-scale embedding computation, while online serving uses low-latency model servers. Privacy is preserved by operating solely on anonymized purchase histories and avoiding personally identifiable features. Incremental updates via online k-means or continual learning maintain freshness as new products enter the catalog. Monitoring focuses on embedding drift detection and periodic re-clustering to sustain cluster quality.

**Challenges and Future Directions**

Key challenges include modality imbalance (sparse images), long-tail product distributions, and cold-start customers with few transactions. Mitigation strategies encompass contrastive pre-training on synthetic pairs and hybrid retrieval that falls back to collaborative signals. Future extensions could incorporate temporal multimodal transformers or reinforcement-learned aggregation policies. Integration with graph neural networks on co-purchase graphs would further enrich relational structure.

**Conclusion**

The proposed embedding-centric pipeline—spanning unimodal encoders, cross-modal fusion, customer aggregation, and neural clustering—offers a scalable, data-driven solution for customer identity resolution and spending-pattern discovery in store sales. By grounding analysis in rich multimodal representations rather than raw transactional features, the approach achieves superior semantic fidelity and generalization. Implementation can commence with off-the-shelf CLIP and Sentence Transformer baselines, followed by domain-specific fine-tuning, delivering measurable improvements in resolution accuracy and downstream personalization within production retail environments.
